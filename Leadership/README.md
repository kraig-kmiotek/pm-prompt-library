## Leadership Blind Spot Identifier

**Use Case:** You experienced a frustrating situation and want to understand what limiting thought pattern might be driving your reaction and how to reframe your thinking.

**Prompt:**

> You are an executive leadership coach. Using only the situation described below, identify the limiting thought pattern most likely driving this leader's frustration and provide reframing questions to help them work through it.
>
> Instructions:
> 1. In 2-3 sentences, name the limiting thought pattern you detect and explain why it fits this situation
> 2. List 4-5 reframing questions the leader can use to challenge that pattern and see the situation differently
> 3. Close with one concrete action the leader could take in the next 24 hours to shift their response
>
> Do not diagnose or pathologize. Frame everything as a normal leadership challenge, not a personal failing.
>
> Situation: [DESCRIBE THE SITUATION IN FREE TEXT - include what happened, who was involved, and what your reaction was]

**Why It Works:** Frustration is often a signal that a limiting belief has been triggered, but most leaders never stop to examine what that belief actually is. Naming the pattern makes it visible and therefore manageable. The reframing questions shift the leader from reaction to reflection, and the 24-hour action prevents the insight from staying theoretical.

## Reframe a Blunt Message

**Use Case:** You have something direct you need to say but want to deliver it in a way that opens dialogue, preserves the relationship, and drives alignment rather than defensiveness.

**Prompt:**

> You are an executive communications coach. Using the message or statement provided below, reframe it so it opens with curiosity, shares with intent, and closes with alignment.
>
> Message type: [WRITTEN or VERBAL - written outputs a reframed message ready to send; verbal outputs a reframed conversational approach with suggested opening lines]
>
> Instructions:
> 1. Open with 1-2 questions that invite the other person's perspective before sharing your own
> 2. Reframe the core message so it is clear and direct without being blunt or one-sided
> 3. Close with a question or statement that invites alignment and shared ownership of next steps
> 4. For written: deliver a complete reframed message ready to send. For verbal: deliver a suggested conversational flow with specific opening lines and key phrases to use
>
> Do not soften the message to the point of losing its meaning. The reframed version should still say what needs to be said.
>
> Original message or statement: [PASTE OR DESCRIBE WHAT YOU WANT TO SAY]

**Why It Works:** Blunt messages often trigger defensiveness before the content even lands. Leading with curiosity signals that you value the other person's perspective, which makes them more receptive to what follows. Closing with alignment shifts the conversation from a verdict to a shared problem, which is where real ownership begins.

## Difficult Feedback Planner

**Use Case:** You need to deliver feedback that is hard to say and potentially hard to hear, and want a structured approach before you walk into the conversation.

**Prompt:**

> You are an executive leadership coach. Using the situation described below, generate a structured approach for delivering this feedback effectively.
>
> Instructions:
> 1. Identify the core message that must land in this conversation, stripped of emotion or judgment
> 2. Recommend how to open the conversation in a way that creates psychological safety without burying the lead
> 3. Outline the key points to hit in a logical order, including what to say, what to listen for, and where to pause and invite response
> 4. Recommend how to close the conversation with clarity on expectations and next steps
> 5. Flag any risks in this specific situation that could derail the conversation and how to prepare for them
>
> Do not generate a word-for-word script. Focus on approach, sequencing, and key points.
>
> Situation: [DESCRIBE THE PERSON, THE SITUATION, AND WHAT YOU NEED THEM TO CHANGE IN FREE TEXT]

**Why It Works:** Most difficult feedback conversations go wrong not because of what is said but because of how it is sequenced and framed. A clear approach before walking in reduces anxiety, keeps the conversation on track, and increases the likelihood the feedback actually changes behavior. Flagging risks specific to the situation prepares the leader for what might push the conversation off course.

## Culture Habit Builder

**Use Case:** You want to embed a specific behavior into your team's daily rhythm and need a concrete plan to trigger, reinforce, and wire it into the culture over time.

**Prompt:**

> You are an executive leadership coach specializing in team culture and behavior change. Using the information provided below, generate a step-by-step plan to embed the desired behavior into this team's culture.
>
> Instructions:
> 1. Identify a natural trigger or existing team routine that can be linked to the desired behavior
> 2. Define the smallest possible version of the behavior the team can start practicing immediately
> 3. Outline a step-by-step 30-day plan to introduce, repeat, and reinforce the behavior
> 4. Recommend how to add emotional weight to the behavior so it sticks, such as recognition, celebration, or shared meaning
> 5. Identify 2-3 early signals that the behavior is taking hold, and 2-3 warning signs it is not
>
> Do not recommend overly complex systems. The plan should be simple enough that a busy leader can execute it without significant overhead.
>
> Current team state: [DESCRIBE THE CURRENT STATE OF YOUR TEAM - include size, dynamic, morale, and any relevant context]
>
> Desired behavior: [DESCRIBE THE SPECIFIC BEHAVIOR YOU WANT YOUR TEAM TO REPEAT]

**Why It Works:** Behaviors become culture through repetition, not mandate. Linking a new behavior to an existing trigger reduces the friction of adoption. Starting small builds early wins that create momentum. The 30-day structure gives the leader a concrete runway without making the effort feel open-ended or overwhelming.
