## Competitive Intelligence Brief from Market Materials

**Use Case:** You have competitive material in any format and want a ready-to-share intelligence brief that surfaces what customer problems a competitor is claiming to solve and who they are optimizing for.

**Prompt:**

> You are a competitive intelligence analyst. Your job is to read between the lines of the material below and produce a structured intelligence brief.
>
> Intended audience: [AUDIENCE - e.g., "product manager," "sales rep," "executive leadership"]
>
> Instructions:
> 1. Write the brief in plain language calibrated for the intended audience
> 2. Open with a 2-3 sentence executive summary covering the competitor's core value proposition and the customer segment they are clearly optimizing for
> 3. Follow with a "Customer Problems They're Targeting" section written as annotated prose. For each problem, note whether the evidence was explicit or implied and which type of source it came from (e.g., marketing copy, spec sheet, homepage)
> 4. Close with a "What to Watch" section: 2-3 sentences on where their positioning is strongest and where it appears weakest or unsubstantiated
>
> Do not infer problems that have no basis in the material. Treat all source types equally regardless of format.
>
> Competitive material: [ATTACH FILES or PASTE CONTENT - accepted formats include URLs, PDFs, spec sheets, and marketing collateral]

**Why It Works:** Competitive material is written to persuade buyers, which means it reveals what problems the market actually recognizes. Structuring the output as an intelligence brief makes it immediately shareable with sales and leadership without translation. The audience variable ensures the language fits who's reading it, not just who ran the prompt.

## Value Proposition Generator

**Use Case:** You have product, customer, or competitive materials and want a structured value proposition that clearly articulates who you're for, what problem you solve, and why you're the right choice.

**Prompt:**

> You are a product strategist. Using the materials provided below, generate a structured value proposition calibrated to the audience specified.
>
> Target audience: [AUDIENCE - e.g., "technical buyer," "business buyer," "executive," "end user"]
>
> Instructions:
> 1. Identify the primary customer and the specific problem or job they are trying to solve
> 2. State the key benefit the product delivers and why it matters to this audience
> 3. Identify the primary alternative or competitor and articulate what makes this product meaningfully different
> 4. Assemble the components into a single, polished value proposition statement in this format: For [customer], who [problem or need], [product name] is a [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [primary alternative], [key differentiator].
> 5. Below the statement, provide a 2-3 sentence explanation of the strategic logic behind the positioning
>
> Do not use buzzwords, superlatives, or vague claims. Every element of the value proposition must be grounded in the materials provided.
>
> Materials: [ATTACH FILES or PASTE CONTENT - product materials, customer research, competitive intel, or any relevant context]

**Why It Works:** Most value propositions fail because they describe what a product does rather than why it matters to a specific customer. Forcing the output into a structured format with a named alternative prevents generic positioning and grounds the statement in competitive reality. The strategic logic section gives the PM language to defend the positioning internally, not just use it externally.

## Market Entry Pressure Test

**Use Case:** You are considering entering a new market or customer segment and want a structured assessment of the key questions you need to answer before committing.

**Prompt:**

> You are a product strategist. Using the market opportunity described below, generate a structured pressure test that surfaces the critical questions and risks the person needs to address before committing to this market.
>
> Instructions:
> 1. Identify the 3-5 most critical assumptions embedded in this market entry thesis
> 2. For each assumption, generate 1-2 sharp questions that must be answered before committing
> 3. Identify the biggest competitive, operational, and timing risks specific to this opportunity
> 4. Flag any signals in the description that suggest this market may be harder to enter than it appears
> 5. Close with a 2-3 sentence summary of what the person needs to know or prove before this opportunity is worth pursuing seriously
>
> Do not evaluate whether the opportunity is good or bad. Focus on surfacing what is unknown or unproven.
>
> Market opportunity: [DESCRIBE THE MARKET OR SEGMENT YOU ARE CONSIDERING IN FREE TEXT - useful context to include: who the target customer is, what problem you believe exists, why you are considering this market now, what your potential advantage is, and any early signals or evidence you have]

**Why It Works:** Most market entry decisions are made on enthusiasm and surface-level research rather than rigorous pressure testing. Separating assumptions from risks forces the PM to distinguish between what they believe and what could go wrong even if they're right. Flagging signals of hidden complexity gives the PM an honest read on whether they're underestimating the difficulty before resources are committed.
